Physiology & Foundations
🟢 FOUNDATIONAL
A 68-year-old man with dilated cardiomyopathy (EF 20%) develops hypotension, cold clammy skin, and oliguria despite being volume-overloaded with bilateral crackles and 3+ pitting edema.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What quadrant of the Forrester matrix is this patient in, and what does that guide therapeutically?
✓ Answer
Wet + Cold (Quadrant III/IV) — cardiogenic shock with congestion. Needs inotropic support + careful diuresis.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The Forrester framework turns two bedside findings — perfusion and congestion — into an immediate treatment decision. This is the conceptual foundation every hemodynamic decision builds on.
🟢 FOUNDATIONAL
A 52-year-old woman presents with palpitations. Her EKG shows a regular narrow complex tachycardia at 160 bpm with no visible P waves. Her BP is 118/76 and she is hemodynamically stable.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Before reaching for adenosine, what is the systematic 5-step EKG interpretation you'd complete — and what does step 3 tell you here?
✓ Answer
Rate→Rhythm→P waves→PR→QRS. Step 3: no visible P waves in a narrow-complex regular tachycardia = AVNRT until proven otherwise.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Students who skip systematic EKG reading make dangerous assumptions. Drilling the 5-step approach as a reflex — not just a memory item — is what separates test-takers from clinicians.
🟢 FOUNDATIONAL
A 24-year-old competitive swimmer has a grade 2/6 systolic ejection murmur at the left upper sternal border that disappears when he stands up. He has no symptoms and a normal EKG.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Is this murmur physiologically innocent or pathological, and how does the maneuver help you decide?
✓ Answer
Innocent — Standing decreases preload → murmur disappears. Pathological murmurs (HCM) get louder with standing.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The single most tested murmur concept on the PANCE is the dynamic maneuver response. Teach this as a framework, not a fact: preload up = most murmurs louder, HCM softer. Preload down = opposite.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
Four patients arrive simultaneously: (A) trauma victim with BP 70/40, HR 130, pale, cool extremities; (B) septic patient with BP 80/50, HR 128, warm flushed skin, bounding pulses; (C) STEMI patient with BP 70/40, HR 110, elevated JVP, bilateral crackles; (D) massive PE patient with BP 75/40, HR 125, elevated JVP, clear lungs.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Classify each patient's shock type using only the bedside exam. What is the single most important hemodynamic distinction between C and D?
✓ Answer
A=Hypovolemic, B=Distributive, C=Cardiogenic, D=Obstructive. C vs D: both have ↑JVP but C has pulmonary edema (LV failure) while D has clear lungs (RV obstruction, can't fill LV).
💡 Teaching Pearl
This vignette should spark 10 minutes of discussion. The C vs D distinction saves lives — students often treat obstructive shock with fluids (harmful) or miss that both look like cardiogenic shock.
🔴 ADVANCED
A 58-year-old diabetic man has 2 hours of 'indigestion' and diaphoresis. His EKG shows a 1mm ST depression in V1-V2 with tall R waves and upright T waves. Initial troponin is negative.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the EKG diagnosis and what is your immediate management — NOT because of the troponin but because of the EKG pattern?
✓ Answer
Posterior STEMI (STEMI equivalent). ST depression V1-V2 + tall R = reciprocal changes of posterior wall injury. Treat as STEMI — emergent cath regardless of negative troponin.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Posterior STEMI is the highest-stakes STEMI equivalent because it looks like 'nothing' on a standard 12-lead. Diabetics blunt chest pain further. This is exactly the vignette that kills patients and kills PANCE scores.
Ischemic Heart Disease
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 61-year-old woman is rushed to the ED with crushing substernal chest pain for 90 minutes. EKG: 4mm ST elevation in V1–V4. BP 96/60, HR 108. She had a hemorrhagic stroke 3 weeks ago.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
PCI is unavailable at your facility. Your fellow suggests lytics. What do you do and why?
✓ Answer
Absolute contraindication to thrombolytics — hemorrhagic stroke within 3 months. Transfer for primary PCI regardless of transfer time. Door-to-balloon ≤90 min is the goal but contraindications override.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Thrombolytic contraindications are high-frequency PANCE questions. Teach them not as a memorization list but as a bleeding risk framework — any recent intracranial event is absolute. The answer here is always transfer.
🟢 FOUNDATIONAL
A 49-year-old smoker has chest pain only during emotional stress and cold exposure. Rest EKG is normal. Exercise stress test is negative. Coronary angiogram shows 20% stenosis in the LAD but transient spasm is induced with ergonovine.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the diagnosis and which drug class is both first-line AND contraindicated?
✓ Answer
Vasospastic (Prinzmetal) angina. CCBs and nitrates are first-line. Beta-blockers are CONTRAINDICATED — can worsen spasm by allowing unopposed alpha-mediated vasoconstriction.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Vasospastic angina is the one case where the beta-blocker reflex kills. Students must burn this into memory: Prinzmetal + beta-blocker = wrong. It's an almost certain PANCE trap when it appears.
Arrhythmias
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 72-year-old man with newly diagnosed AFib (first detected 36 hours ago) is rate-controlled with metoprolol. His CHA₂DS₂-VASc score is 4. You plan cardioversion.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What must happen before cardioversion, and why does the 48-hour window matter physiologically?
✓ Answer
If AFib duration unknown or ≥48h: TEE to rule out LAA thrombus OR 3 weeks therapeutic anticoagulation before cardioversion. After ≥48h, thrombus can form in LAA — cardioversion dislodges it → stroke.
💡 Teaching Pearl
This is one of the most tested clinical sequences in cardiology. The 48-hour rule is not arbitrary — teach the physiology: LAA stasis → thrombus → mechanical stunning post-cardioversion keeps risk elevated, so 4 weeks anticoag after is also required.
🔴 ADVANCED
A 44-year-old with palpitations has a regular wide-complex tachycardia at 186 bpm. He is hemodynamically stable. His baseline EKG (brought from home) shows a delta wave and short PR interval.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Why is adenosine potentially lethal here, and what do you give instead?
✓ Answer
WPW with antidromic SVT (or pre-excited AFib). Adenosine blocks the AV node → all conduction goes down the accessory pathway → can precipitate VF. Give procainamide (slows accessory pathway). If unstable → cardiovert.
💡 Teaching Pearl
WPW is the PANCE's favorite trap in arrhythmia questions. The teaching point is simple: when you see a delta wave, every decision about AV-nodal agents (adenosine, digoxin, CCBs, beta-blockers) changes. Procainamide is the safe drug.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 78-year-old woman is found to have a PR interval of 280ms on a routine EKG. She is asymptomatic. One week later she develops complete heart block with a ventricular escape rate of 38 bpm and syncopal episodes.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
1st degree → 3rd degree: what happened anatomically, and what is the only treatment?
✓ Answer
Progression from AV nodal delay (1st degree) to complete block (3rd degree) — likely fibrosis of conduction system. 3rd degree block with symptoms = permanent pacemaker. Atropine is temporizing bridge only.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Heart block progression is favorite PANCE territory. Key teaching: 1st degree alone never needs treatment. 2nd degree Mobitz II and 3rd degree almost always need pacing. Drill the escapes: junctional 40-60, ventricular 20-40.
Heart Failure & Cardiomyopathies
🔴 ADVANCED
A 65-year-old man with HFrEF (EF 30%) is on carvedilol and lisinopril. He is NYHA class II, stable. You want to optimize his GDMT. His K+ is 4.2, eGFR 58. He has no history of hospitalization for HF.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Name the two remaining GDMT drug classes he should be on and what you must check before starting each.
✓ Answer
SGLT2i (dapagliflozin/empagliflozin — check eGFR ≥20, ok here) + MRA (spironolactone — check K+ <5.0 and eGFR ≥30, ok here). Ideally upgrade ACEi to ARNI (sacubitril/valsartan) for further mortality benefit.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The 4-pillar GDMT framework is now THE most important HF management concept on the PANCE. Students must know all 4 pillars, the contraindications, and the safety labs for each. This vignette forces them to apply it rather than recall it.
🔺 TRAP LEVEL
A 17-year-old basketball player collapses during practice. He is resuscitated from ventricular fibrillation. Family history: his uncle died suddenly at age 28 during a football game. Echo shows LV wall thickness of 22mm with systolic anterior motion of the mitral valve.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the diagnosis, what is definitive management for this patient, and what is the #1 thing NOT to give him?
✓ Answer
HCM with SCD — ICD implantation for secondary prevention. Do NOT give digoxin, nitrates, or pure afterload reducers (vasodilators) — all decrease preload/afterload → worsen LVOTO → hemodynamic collapse.
💡 Teaching Pearl
This vignette hits multiple board targets at once: young athlete + SCD = HCM until proven otherwise, family history = autosomal dominant, SAM = hallmark echo finding. The drug contraindications are the trap. Beta-blockers and CCBs are correct; digoxin and nitrates are lethal.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 42-year-old man with a history of alcohol use (1 pint of whiskey daily for 15 years) presents with progressive dyspnea and bilateral leg edema. Echo shows EF of 18%, 4-chamber dilation, and moderate MR.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Name the most likely etiology, the one intervention most likely to improve EF, and the most important prognostic factor.
✓ Answer
Alcoholic (toxic) dilated cardiomyopathy. Complete alcohol cessation can recover EF significantly — potentially the only reversible CMP. Most important prognostic factor: whether EF improves with cessation vs standard GDMT.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Dilated CMP vignettes test whether students can identify reversible causes: alcohol, cocaine, peripartum, tachycardia-mediated, iron overload. Alcoholic DCM is the highest-yield reversible cause — cessation is both treatment and prognosis.
🔺 TRAP LEVEL
A 71-year-old man with multiple myeloma has progressive dyspnea and lower extremity edema. Echo shows markedly thickened LV walls (17mm), a 'sparkling' myocardial texture, EF 42%, and Grade III diastolic dysfunction. His EKG shows low voltage throughout despite the thick walls.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the diagnosis, what drug is absolutely contraindicated, and what ATTR-specific therapy was FDA-approved in 2019?
✓ Answer
Cardiac amyloidosis (AL type given MM). Digoxin CONTRAINDICATED — amyloid fibrils bind digoxin → toxicity at therapeutic levels. Tafamidis (Vyndamax) — transthyretin stabilizer, FDA 2019 for ATTR (wild-type and hereditary).
💡 Teaching Pearl
The low-voltage + thick walls paradox is pathognomonic — hypertrophy causes high voltage; infiltration does not. The digoxin contraindication is the board trap that tests whether students remember the mechanism, not just the rule. Tafamidis is the newest testable therapy.
Valvular & Pericardial Disease
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 78-year-old woman has exertional syncope, angina, and dyspnea on climbing one flight of stairs. Exam reveals a harsh crescendo-decrescendo murmur at the right upper sternal border with radiation to the carotids. Her aortic valve area is 0.7 cm².
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the diagnosis and when is this a surgical emergency versus elective repair?
✓ Answer
Severe aortic stenosis (AVA <1.0 cm²). The triad of syncope/angina/HF = severe symptomatic AS. Once symptoms develop, median survival is 2–3 years without intervention. TAVR or SAVR depending on surgical risk.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The AS triad (SAD: Syncope, Angina, Dyspnea) plus their survival statistics is pure PANCE gold. Syncope = 3 yr survival, Angina = 5 yr, Dyspnea = 1 yr. Symptoms = the valve MUST be fixed.
🔺 TRAP LEVEL
A 28-year-old IV drug user has a 5-day history of fever, back pain, and new confusion. Blood cultures grow MRSA. Echo shows a large (1.2 cm) tricuspid valve vegetation with moderate tricuspid regurgitation.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What are the Duke criteria findings here, what are three surgical indications, and why is this patient's neurologic change particularly alarming?
✓ Answer
Definite IE: 2 major criteria (positive blood cultures + echo vegetation). Surgical indications: large vegetation >1cm + embolic events, HF, persistent bacteremia. New confusion = septic emboli to brain or mycotic aneurysm = emergent imaging.
💡 Teaching Pearl
IE vignettes test three layers: 1) Duke criteria recognition, 2) surgical indications, 3) which bugs go with which patients. IVDU → right-sided → tricuspid → Staph aureus. This vignette adds the embolic complication layer that separates scores.
🔴 ADVANCED
A 45-year-old with newly diagnosed lung adenocarcinoma develops progressive dyspnea over 3 days. BP 84/60, HR 128, JVD present, lungs clear. Heart sounds are muffled. Pulsus paradoxus is 18 mmHg. Bedside echo shows a large pericardial effusion with RV diastolic collapse.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the immediate life-saving intervention, and why is the pulsus paradoxus physiologically significant here?
✓ Answer
Emergent pericardiocentesis. Pulsus paradoxus >10 mmHg = cardiac tamponade physiology — exaggerated ventricular interdependence. During inspiration, RV filling compresses LV (fixed pericardial space) → drop in systolic BP >10.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Beck's triad (hypotension + JVD + muffled heart sounds) + pulsus paradoxus + echo RV collapse = perfect tamponade vignette. The physiology explanation takes 2 minutes and locks in the concept permanently. This is where your SVG tamponade diagram earns its place.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 19-year-old college student presents with 5 days of chest pain that worsens when lying flat and improves leaning forward. He had a URI 2 weeks ago. Troponin is 3.2 ng/mL. EKG shows diffuse ST elevation with PR depression.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
The cardiology fellow says 'rule out STEMI.' How do you differentiate this from STEMI at the bedside and on EKG?
✓ Answer
Myopericarditis. STEMI: localized ST elevation, reciprocal changes, no PR depression. Myopericarditis: diffuse ST elevation, PR depression (pathognomonic), positional pain, young patient, viral prodrome, no reciprocal depression in aVR.
💡 Teaching Pearl
This is one of the highest-impact teaching vignettes — students will encounter this and misdiagnose it as STEMI. Teach the EKG differences explicitly: saddle-shaped ST, PR depression in multiple leads, no reciprocal changes. The troponin rise is from myocarditis, not ischemia.
Hypertension & Shock
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 55-year-old man presents with BP 220/130, blurry vision, and severe headache. Fundoscopic exam shows papilledema and flame hemorrhages. His creatinine has risen from 0.9 to 2.4 in 24 hours. He is alert and oriented.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What distinguishes this from a hypertensive urgency, and what is your first drug and target BP in the first hour?
✓ Answer
Hypertensive emergency — end-organ damage present (retinopathy, AKI). Urgency = no end-organ damage. Target: reduce MAP by no more than 25% in first hour (avoid overcorrection = watershed infarct). IV nicardipine, labetalol, or clevidipine.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The most important PANCE teaching point here: the target is NOT normal BP. 25% MAP reduction in first hour, then gradual to 160/100 over 6h. Students who slam the pressure to normal cause strokes. The 25% rule is testable and clinically critical.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 33-year-old woman with known anaphylaxis to bee stings is stung at a picnic. Within minutes: BP 62/40, HR 142, diffuse urticaria, stridor. She has no epinephrine auto-injector.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Name the mechanism of shock, the first drug and route, and what you do if she gets epinephrine but BP doesn't improve.
✓ Answer
Distributive (anaphylactic) shock — massive histamine/IgE-mediated vasodilation. Epinephrine 1:1000, 0.3mg IM lateral thigh — FIRST. If refractory: IV fluids, diphenhydramine, ranitidine, methylprednisolone, and possibly vasopressors.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Anaphylaxis vignettes test drug selection AND route. Epinephrine IM is faster than SQ. Students frequently give antihistamines first — that's wrong. Epinephrine is the only drug that reverses all pathways simultaneously.
Vascular Disease
🔺 TRAP LEVEL
A 62-year-old hypertensive man has sudden tearing chest pain radiating to his back. BP right arm 178/96, left arm 142/84. CXR shows widened mediastinum. He is on an NSAID for gout and takes amlodipine.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the diagnosis, what imaging confirms it, what do you do in the first 5 minutes for BP, and which surgical decision hinges entirely on location?
✓ Answer
Type A aortic dissection (ascending). CT angiography chest. BP target SBP <120 AND HR <60 — IV esmolol + nicardipine. Type A = emergency surgery. Type B = medical management unless complications (ischemia, rupture, uncontrolled pain).
💡 Teaching Pearl
Dissection vignettes always test: (1) the BP differential, (2) Stanford classification, (3) anti-impulse therapy sequence (HR first, then BP), (4) Type A = OR, Type B = medical. Each of these is independently testable. This is a 4-point question disguised as one vignette.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 38-year-old woman on oral contraceptives presents with 3 days of pleuritic chest pain and dyspnea. HR 112, SpO₂ 92%, BP 118/74. Wells score is 6. EKG shows sinus tachycardia.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Without waiting for CT results, what risk-stratification step tells you whether to give anticoagulation now, and what class of drug is first-line?
✓ Answer
Wells >4 = high probability — start anticoagulation before imaging (unless absolute contraindication). DOACs (rivaroxaban or apixaban) are first-line — no parenteral bridge needed. CT-PA is confirmatory, not gating.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The PANCE tests whether students know when to treat before confirming. High Wells = treat empirically while confirming. Teaching the Wells algorithm as a sequential decision tree (not a memorization exercise) is what makes this stick.
🟢 FOUNDATIONAL
A 68-year-old smoker complains of bilateral calf cramping after walking 2 blocks that resolves with rest. His ABI is 0.62 in the right leg and 0.71 in the left.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the diagnosis, what does the ABI value tell you about severity, and what is the first-line medical intervention?
✓ Answer
Peripheral artery disease — intermittent claudication. ABI 0.41–0.90 = mild-moderate PAD (normal ≥1.0, critical ischemia <0.4). First-line: supervised walking exercise program + antiplatelet therapy (aspirin or clopidogrel) + statin.
💡 Teaching Pearl
ABI is the PANCE's favorite PAD diagnostic. Teach the cutoffs: >1.4 = non-compressible (DM/calcified), 0.91-1.4 = normal, 0.41-0.90 = PAD, <0.4 = critical ischemia. Exercise therapy is consistently undertested by students who jump to revascularization.
Gap Topics
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 52-year-old woman with T2DM, no history of ASCVD, LDL 148, is started on atorvastatin 40mg. Six weeks later she reports severe bilateral leg pain. CK is 8,400 U/L. She was recently started on diltiazem for AFib.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the diagnosis, what is the drug interaction, and what do you do immediately?
✓ Answer
Statin myopathy/rhabdomyolysis. Diltiazem inhibits CYP3A4 → ↑ atorvastatin levels → myotoxicity. Stop atorvastatin immediately + aggressive IV hydration (myoglobinuria → AKI). Switch to rosuvastatin or pravastatin (less CYP3A4 dependent) when restarting.
💡 Teaching Pearl
CYP3A4 statin interactions are one of the most tested drug-drug interactions on the PANCE. Teach the non-CYP3A4 statins (rosuvastatin, pravastatin, pitavastatin) as the safe alternatives. The clinical consequence — rhabdomyolysis and AKI — is why it matters.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 6-week-old infant is brought in for poor feeding and diaphoresis with feedings. Exam: weight gain suboptimal, HR 168, RR 52, hepatomegaly. A holosystolic murmur is heard at the LLSB. CXR shows cardiomegaly and increased pulmonary vascular markings.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the most likely diagnosis, and why does this infant have heart failure — given that the shunt is LEFT-to-RIGHT?
✓ Answer
Large VSD. L→R shunt → pulmonary overcirculation → pulmonary HTN → increased pulmonary venous return → LV volume overload → HF. Feeding difficulty is the HF equivalent in infants (metabolic demand + increased work of breathing).
💡 Teaching Pearl
The L→R → HF paradox trips up every class. Teaching the physiology (shunt overwhelms pulmonary circulation → volume overload) is more durable than the fact alone. Feeding difficulty = HF in infants is a pattern recognition gem.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 65-year-old woman has episodic palpitations followed by dizziness. Her Holter shows alternating periods of sinus bradycardia (38 bpm) and paroxysmal AFib at 168 bpm. Her cardiologist wants to start flecainide for the AFib.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Why is starting antiarrhythmic therapy without a pacemaker potentially dangerous in this patient?
✓ Answer
Brady-tachy syndrome (SSS). Antiarrhythmics that suppress the tachycardia will also suppress the SA node further → prolonged pauses → syncope/asystole. Must place pacemaker FIRST, then add antiarrhythmic therapy safely.
💡 Teaching Pearl
This is the classic SSS management trap — treating only one half of the problem. The teaching point is: you cannot add rate-slowing or rhythm-suppressing drugs to a sick sinus without backup pacing. Holter showing alternating brady-tachy = SSS until proven otherwise.
🟢 FOUNDATIONAL
A 78-year-old man on tamsulosin for BPH, lisinopril, and furosemide has a witnessed fall in his bathroom in the morning after rising from bed. He denies pre-syncope but his wife reports he stood up, took two steps, and went down. BP lying: 142/88. BP standing (1 min): 104/62.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the diagnosis, which of his medications is most responsible, and what do you change?
✓ Answer
Orthostatic hypotension — SBP drop ≥20 (here 38 mmHg). Tamsulosin (alpha-1 blocker) most responsible — causes vasodilation on standing. Discontinue tamsulosin or switch to a uroselective alpha-blocker with lower vasodilation risk. Also review furosemide dose.
💡 Teaching Pearl
This is a classic geriatric PANCE vignette — most falls in the elderly have a medication cause. Teaching students to look at the medication list before ordering a tilt table is fundamental primary care. Alpha-blockers are the most overlooked cause of orthostatic hypotension.

Obstructive Disease
🟢 FOUNDATIONAL
A 22-year-old female college student with asthma uses her albuterol inhaler 4 times per week and wakes up with symptoms twice a month. She has had no exacerbations requiring oral steroids.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What GINA severity level is this, and what is her controller regimen according to 2024 guidelines — including the paradigm shift in rescue therapy?
✓ Answer
Mild persistent asthma (Step 2). GINA 2024: ICS-formoterol PRN (single maintenance and reliever therapy — SMART) is preferred over SABA alone. SABA-only therapy is no longer recommended even for mild asthma.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The SABA paradigm shift is the single most important 2024 PANCE update in pulmonology. Students trained before 2022 know the old algorithm. Teach the new explicitly: every asthmatic with symptoms gets ICS-containing therapy. SABA alone increases mortality.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 66-year-old male smoker with COPD presents with worsening dyspnea and increased yellow sputum for 3 days. SpO₂ is 81% on room air. You apply high-flow oxygen and bring his SpO₂ to 99%. Thirty minutes later he is drowsier and a repeat ABG shows pCO₂ 68 mmHg (baseline 52).
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What physiologic mechanism explains the CO₂ rise, and what should the SpO₂ target have been?
✓ Answer
Hypoxic drive suppression + Haldane effect — high O₂ eliminates hypoxic drive in a CO₂ retainer → hypoventilation → hypercapnia. Target SpO₂ 88–92% in COPD exacerbation. Titrate O₂ down; consider BiPAP.
💡 Teaching Pearl
This is THE most tested oxygenation trap in pulmonology. The instinct to correct hypoxia is right; the instinct to target normal SpO₂ in a COPD exacerbation is wrong. Teaching the physiologic mechanism (not just the rule) prevents this error in clinical practice.
Diagnostic
🟢 FOUNDATIONAL
A 58-year-old never-smoker woman has progressive exertional dyspnea. PFTs: FVC 58% predicted, FEV1 62% predicted, FEV1/FVC 0.86, TLC 62% predicted, DLCO 41% predicted.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What PFT pattern is this, what does the ↓DLCO tell you specifically, and what are the two most likely diagnoses?
✓ Answer
Restrictive pattern (↓FVC + ↓TLC + normal/elevated FEV1/FVC). ↓DLCO = impaired alveolar gas exchange → parenchymal disease, NOT just mechanical restriction. Most likely: IPF or pulmonary hypertension.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The DLCO is the forgotten PFT variable that changes the diagnosis. Normal DLCO with restriction = extrapulmonary (neuromuscular, obesity). Low DLCO with restriction = parenchymal (ILD, pulmonary HTN). Teaching the DLCO layer separates superficial PFT interpretation from the real clinical skill.
Infectious & Inflammatory
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 71-year-old nursing home resident with DM2 presents with fever, productive cough, and right lower lobe consolidation on CXR. CURB-65 score is 3. She is started on azithromycin monotherapy.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Is this regimen appropriate, and if not, what is the correct treatment — and why does her living situation matter?
✓ Answer
Inappropriate. CURB-65 ≥3 = severe → inpatient treatment. Nursing home resident = healthcare-associated risk → coverage for gram-negatives and possibly MRSA. Correct: respiratory fluoroquinolone (levofloxacin) or beta-lactam + macrolide. Consider HCAP protocol.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The CAP vignette teaches two decisions simultaneously: severity scoring drives inpatient vs outpatient, and patient context drives antimicrobial selection. Most students correctly identify the CURB-65 threshold but miss that nursing home residency changes coverage.
🔴 ADVANCED
A 34-year-old refugee from Southeast Asia has a positive IGRA. CXR shows a left upper lobe nodule with calcification and no infiltrates. He has no symptoms. HIV test is negative. He was previously treated for TB 12 years ago and reports he completed a 'short course.'
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is your management of latent TB here — and what complicates the decision given his treatment history?
✓ Answer
Latent TB treatment indicated (positive IGRA + untreated/inadequately treated). Options: INH × 9 months, or 3HP (INH + rifapentine weekly × 12 weeks — preferred for adherence), or 4R (rifampin × 4 months). Prior treatment complicates — if truly completed, may not benefit; if incomplete, retreatment indicated. Verify records.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Latent TB treatment options were updated and 3HP is now preferred by CDC for most patients. The prior treatment complication is clinical reality — many refugees have incomplete records. A 60-year-old woman is found to have a 2-cm cystic lesion in the pancreatic body on CT done for another reason. The cyst has a central scar with 'sunburst' calcification.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the most likely diagnosis?
✓ Answer
Serous cystadenoma.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Serous cystadenomas are benign pancreatic cysts, more common in older women, with a characteristic central scar and 'sunburst' calcification, and virtually no malignant potential — they can be observed. Mucinous cystic neoplasms and IPMNs, by contrast, carry malignant potential.
🔴 ADVANCED
A patient with acute pancreatitis has on admission: BUN 28 mg/dL, HR 110, and a rising BUN at 24 hours.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What does this indicate?
✓ Answer
Increased risk of mortality and a need for aggressive fluid resuscitation.
💡 Teaching Pearl
A BUN >20 mg/dL on admission and any rise within 24 hours are associated with significantly increased mortality in acute pancreatitis. BUN reflects intravascular volume status and is incorporated into severity scores (e.g., BISAP).

🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 25-year-old woman presents with chronic diarrhea, bloating, and iron deficiency anemia. She has a family history of celiac disease.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the initial screening test?
✓ Answer
Tissue transglutaminase IgA (tTG-IgA) antibody, with a total serum IgA level.
💡 Teaching Pearl
tTG-IgA is the preferred serologic screen for celiac disease; total IgA is checked simultaneously because IgA deficiency causes false-negative results. 🟢 FOUNDATIONAL
A 30-year-old woman is newly diagnosed with celiac disease.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the treatment, and what associated condition should be screened for?

Strict lifelong gluten-free diet; screen for associated autoimmune conditions, especially thyroid disease (Hashimoto) and type 1 diabetes.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Celiac disease is associated with dermatitis herpetiformis, type 1 diabetes, autoimmune thyroid disease, and IgA deficiency. Patients should also be assessed for nutritional deficiencies (iron, folate, vitamin D, calcium, B12).

Systemic Syndromes
🔴 ADVANCED
A 68-year-old nursing home patient arrives obtunded with BP 74/44, HR 128, T 39.4°C, RR 28, and lactate 5.2 mmol/L. Blood cultures are drawn. You want to start broad-spectrum antibiotics — but the ICU attending says 'we need a CT first to find the source.'
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Who is right, and walk through the SSC 2021 Hour-1 Bundle in order — including the one recommendation that was DOWNGRADED from the 2018 guidelines.
✓ Answer
You are right — antibiotics within 1 hour of septic shock recognition. CT after antibiotics, not before. Bundle: (1) Lactate, (2) Blood cultures × 2, (3) Antibiotics, (4) 30 mL/kg crystalloid (DOWNGRADED to weak recommendation in 2021 — use dynamic measures), (5) Norepinephrine if hypotensive despite fluids.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The 2021 SSC update is a major board topic — the 30 mL/kg downgrade matters clinically (fluid overload kills septic patients too). More importantly: antibiotics NEVER wait for imaging. One-hour delay in antibiotics = ~7% mortality increase. The CT can follow.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 58-year-old man with COPD is admitted with productive cough, fever, and a right lower lobe infiltrate. He is saturating 91% on room air. He is not on ICU-level support. His CURB-65 score is 2.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What antibiotic regimen is appropriate, and what is the most important paradigm shift regarding aspiration pneumonia coverage?
✓ Answer
CURB-65 2 = inpatient non-ICU. Regimen: beta-lactam (ceftriaxone) + macrolide OR respiratory fluoroquinolone alone. Paradigm shift: aspiration pneumonia does NOT routinely need anaerobic coverage (metronidazole) — IDSA 2019 data shows metronidazole increases mortality. Use standard CAP antibiotics.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The aspiration pneumonia antibiotics paradigm shift is the highest-yield ID update in this topic. Students who trained before 2019 reflexively add metronidazole or clindamycin for aspiration — that is now wrong per IDSA guidelines. Standard CAP regimen is correct.
🟢 FOUNDATIONAL
A 24-year-old healthy woman has 2 days of dysuria, urinary frequency, and urgency. She is afebrile. UA: positive leukocyte esterase, nitrites, WBC 30–40/hpf, bacteria +3. She is not pregnant.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the diagnosis, first-line treatment, and what would make you choose a different antibiotic than your first choice?
✓ Answer
Uncomplicated cystitis. First-line: nitrofurantoin × 5 days or TMP-SMX × 3 days (if local resistance <20%). Avoid nitrofurantoin if: CrCl <30 (no renal parenchymal penetration, won't work) or pyelonephritis (same reason — bladder drug only). Avoid TMP-SMX if local E. coli resistance >20%.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The nitrofurantoin limitation is the most tested prescribing trap in UTI management. Students who choose nitrofurantoin for pyelonephritis give a drug that cannot reach the kidney parenchyma. Teaching the pharmacokinetics (concentrated in urine, not renal tissue) is more durable than the rule alone.
CNS & Endovascular
🔺 TRAP LEVEL
A 19-year-old college student has 6 hours of fever, severe headache, neck stiffness, and photophobia. He is confused. On exam: non-blanching petechiae on his legs and trunk. BP 94/56.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Before the CT scanner is available, in what sequence do you act — and why does the dexamethasone timing relative to antibiotics matter critically?
✓ Answer
1) Blood cultures × 2 immediately, 2) Dexamethasone 0.15 mg/kg IV, 3) Ceftriaxone 2g IV + vancomycin — ALL within 30 minutes, 4) CT if papilledema or focal neuro deficits present, 5) LP after CT if no herniation risk. Dexamethasone BEFORE or WITH first antibiotic dose only — if given after, anti-inflammatory benefit is lost. Petechiae = meningococcemia → also give rifampin prophylaxis to contacts.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The dexamethasone timing trap is answered perfectly by understanding the mechanism — dex works by blocking cytokine release triggered by bacterial lysis from antibiotics. If you give dex after the first antibiotic dose, the lysis has already occurred and benefit is lost. This mechanism-based teaching prevents memorization errors.
🔴 ADVANCED
A 45-year-old man with a prosthetic aortic valve (placed 3 years ago) develops fever, night sweats, and malaise over 3 weeks. Blood cultures grow coagulase-negative Staphylococcus epidermidis × 3/3 bottles. Echo shows a new 8mm oscillating mass on the prosthetic valve.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Apply Duke criteria. What is the organism's significance given prosthetic valve timing — and what drug combination is required for this pathogen on a prosthetic valve?
✓ Answer
Definite IE: 2 major criteria (positive cultures × 2 for typical organism + echo vegetation). S. epidermidis on a prosthetic valve >2 months post-op = late PVE — this organism (normally low virulence) is the most common cause of late PVE. Treatment: vancomycin + rifampin × ≥6 weeks + gentamicin × 2 weeks (rifampin prevents biofilm formation on prosthetic material).
💡 Teaching Pearl
Late prosthetic valve IE teaches two high-yield concepts: the organism-timing association (early PVE = Staph aureus from surgical contamination; late PVE = Staph epidermidis from seeding) and the rifampin addition for prosthetic material. Rifampin's biofilm penetration mechanism is the reason for triple therapy — worth teaching.
STIs & GI Infections
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 22-year-old sexually active woman has vaginal discharge, dyspareunia, and lower abdominal pain for 3 days. Pelvic exam: cervical motion tenderness, adnexal tenderness bilaterally, uterine tenderness. Pregnancy test is negative. Cervical NAAT is pending.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the diagnosis and current CDC 2021 recommended regimen — including the specific change from prior guidelines?
✓ Answer
PID. CDC 2021 regimen: ceftriaxone 500mg IM × 1 + doxycycline 100mg BID × 14 days + metronidazole 500mg BID × 14 days. Key 2021 change: ceftriaxone dose increased from 250mg to 500mg (due to declining cephalosporin susceptibility in gonorrhea). Azithromycin is no longer recommended for gonorrhea (resistance).
💡 Teaching Pearl
The CDC 2021 STI guideline changes are high-frequency PANCE updates. The dose increase (250→500mg ceftriaxone) and the removal of azithromycin from gonorrhea regimens are the two most testable changes. Students who memorized the old guidelines will get these wrong.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 72-year-old hospitalized patient has 8 watery stools per day for 4 days following a 10-day course of clindamycin. C. diff toxin PCR is positive. WBC 24,000. Temperature 38.9°C. He is alert and hemodynamically stable.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What severity is this, and what is the first-line treatment per current IDSA guidelines — including the paradigm shift away from metronidazole?
✓ Answer
Severe CDI (WBC ≥15,000 or Cr rise ≥1.5×). First-line: oral vancomycin 125mg QID × 10 days OR fidaxomicin 200mg BID × 10 days. Metronidazole is NO LONGER first-line for any CDI severity (IDSA 2021) — higher failure rates vs vancomycin. Fidaxomicin preferred when recurrence prevention is priority.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The metronidazole demotion in C. diff is the single most important CDI update — and it was such a major change that boards immediately started testing it. Students trained before 2018 still default to metronidazole. Fidaxomicin's superiority for recurrence prevention (especially with fidaxomicin's unique narrow spectrum) is the advanced teaching layer.
Immunocompromised & Special
🔴 ADVANCED
A 32-year-old man newly diagnosed with HIV has a CD4 count of 68 and HIV RNA of 240,000 copies/mL. He has a 2-week history of headache, fever, and neck stiffness. CSF opening pressure is 340 mmH₂O, lymphocytes 18, glucose 38, protein 90, India ink +.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the diagnosis, what is the urgent procedure that must be done immediately regardless of treatment, and what does TMP-SMX cover for this patient that he still needs?
✓ Answer
Cryptococcal meningitis. Urgent therapeutic LP — high ICP is the primary cause of death. Target opening pressure <20 cmH₂O. Treatment: liposomal amphotericin B + flucytosine × 2 weeks (induction) → fluconazole (consolidation/maintenance). TMP-SMX still covers PCP AND Toxoplasma prophylaxis — start after cryptococcal treatment.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Therapeutic LP in cryptococcal meningitis is the most overlooked acute intervention. Students know to give antifungals — they miss that ICP management saves the life. The daily LPs until pressure normalizes is the unique management feature. The TMP-SMX question tests whether students remember the CD4-based prophylaxis table.
🟢 FOUNDATIONAL
A 68-year-old woman with COPD presents on day 2 of influenza-like illness — fever, myalgia, cough. Rapid influenza test is positive. She is afebrile at the office but appears fatigued. Her daughter also has flu symptoms.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Does the 48-hour oseltamivir window still apply here, and what are the indications for treatment regardless of symptom duration?
✓ Answer
Yes — start oseltamivir if within 48h of symptom onset AND patient has high-risk features. High-risk always treat regardless of timing: age ≥65, COPD, immunocompromised, pregnancy, severe illness, hospitalized. Oseltamivir reduces duration ~1 day and severity. Daughter: test and treat if symptomatic (not post-exposure prophylaxis — she has symptoms).
💡 Teaching Pearl
The 48-hour rule has exceptions — and those exceptions are what the PANCE tests. High-risk patients benefit from oseltamivir even if past 48 hours. Teaching the high-risk list (COPD, elderly, immunocompromised, pregnant) as the clinical decision driver is more useful than the time rule alone.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 61-year-old man with hypertension and DM2 tests positive for COVID-19 on day 2 of mild symptoms (fatigue, rhinorrhea, low-grade fever). He is not hospitalized. His eGFR is 42, and he is on simvastatin 40mg.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Is Paxlovid indicated, and what drug interaction must you address before prescribing?
✓ Answer
Paxlovid (nirmatrelvir/ritonavir) indicated — high-risk outpatient, within 5 days of symptoms, not hospitalized. Drug interaction: ritonavir is a strong CYP3A4 inhibitor → simvastatin levels increase dramatically → rhabdomyolysis risk. Hold simvastatin during Paxlovid course (5 days). eGFR 42 requires dose adjustment (30-60 range gets reduced dose; <30 = avoid).
💡 Teaching Pearl
Paxlovid's ritonavir interactions are the most testable COVID pharmacology topic. The CYP3A4 inhibition affects dozens of drugs (statins, certain antihypertensives, immunosuppressants, anticoagulants). Teaching the mechanism rather than just the drug list enables students to reason about novel combinations they haven't memorized.
🔴 ADVANCED
A 26-year-old HIV-positive man (CD4 44, not on ART) from Arizona develops fever, weight loss, and a persistent non-productive cough for 3 weeks. CXR shows bilateral diffuse nodular infiltrates. BAL shows spherules with endospores. Serum Coccidioides IgM is positive.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the treatment, and how does management differ from the same infection in an immunocompetent person?
✓ Answer
Disseminated coccidioidomycosis. Immunocompetent mild disease: fluconazole × 3–6 months OR observation. HIV/immunocompromised with dissemination: fluconazole 400mg/day lifelong (or until CD4 >250 on ART). If CNS involvement or severe dissemination: amphotericin B + azole. Start ART urgently.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The geographic fungi (Histo, Cocci, Blasto) are favorite PANCE topic because they're location-based pattern recognition. Arizona/Southwest = Cocci. Ohio/Mississippi Valley = Histo. Central US/SE = Blasto. The HIV modifier makes management more aggressive and lifelong. Teaching location → organism is a clinical shortcut that works.
🔺 TRAP LEVEL
A 42-year-old woman in Connecticut had a tick bite 6 weeks ago and a self-resolving rash she described as 'like a target.' Now she has bilateral facial droop. Her ELISA for Lyme is positive; Western blot is positive for IgG only (IgM negative).
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Interpret the serology, explain the significance of bilateral vs unilateral facial palsy, and determine whether her Lyme is early or late disseminated.
✓ Answer
IgG only (no IgM) = chronic/late infection — IgM is most sensitive in early disease, wanes by 4–6 weeks. IgG rises in disseminated disease. Bilateral facial palsy = pathognomonic for Lyme disease (unilateral = many causes). This is early disseminated Lyme (Stage 2 = weeks to months). Treatment: oral doxycycline × 14–21 days (IV ceftriaxone only if meningitis/cardiac).
💡 Teaching Pearl
The serology timing interpretation (IgM early → fades, IgG late → persists) is a PANCE pattern recognition skill. The bilateral facial palsy teaching point is memorable and high-yield — bilateral = Lyme until proven otherwise. Students often default to IV ceftriaxone for all neuro Lyme, but isolated facial nerve palsy is oral doxy.

🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 10-year-old boy has a 2-week history of fever, painful swollen knees that resolved and then his wrists became swollen, and the school nurse noticed he has been making involuntary arm movements during class. His throat culture was positive for GAS 3 weeks ago.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
Apply the Jones criteria, and what is the long-term treatment that must be started today?
✓ Answer
Meets Jones criteria: 2 major (migratory polyarthritis + Sydenham chorea) + evidence of preceding GAS = definite ARF. Start secondary prophylaxis immediately: benzathine PCN G 1.2 MU IM now and then q4 weeks. Chorea can be the sole presentation — it confirms active rheumatic fever.
💡 Teaching Pearl
This vignette teaches the migratory arthritis pattern (pain LEAVES one joint and MOVES TO another — not additive) and Sydenham chorea as a standalone major criterion. The secondary prophylaxis must start NOW — not after full evaluation — because the risk of recurrence from any strep exposure begins immediately.
🔺 TRAP LEVEL
A 20-year-old college student has severe pharyngitis and is prescribed amoxicillin by urgent care. Three days later he develops a diffuse maculopapular rash covering his trunk, abdomen, and extremities. His CBC shows 58% lymphocytes with 22% atypical lymphocytes. Monospot is positive.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What caused the rash, is this a penicillin allergy, and what ONE activity restriction must be given before he leaves your office?
✓ Answer
EBV-induced amoxicillin rash — pharmacologic reaction, NOT IgE-mediated penicillin allergy. Do NOT label as penicillin allergic. Activity restriction: NO contact sports for ≥3-4 weeks (splenomegaly risk → splenic rupture). This is the most important immediate clinical action.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Three teachable moments in one vignette: the amoxicillin rash ≠ penicillin allergy (patients get labeled allergic for life incorrectly), the atypical lymphocytes are reactive T cells (not EBV-infected B cells), and the contact sports restriction is the intervention that prevents the most dangerous complication. The restriction often gets forgotten in the excitement of making the diagnosis.
🔴 ADVANCED
A 72-year-old immunosuppressed transplant patient on tacrolimus develops a vesicular rash in the V1 trigeminal distribution (forehead, tip of nose). She reports visual blurring.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the significance of the nasal tip involvement, what is the urgent consultation needed, and what are the two systemic risks in this immunosuppressed host that differ from an immunocompetent patient?
✓ Answer
Hutchinson's sign (nasal tip vesicles) = nasociliary nerve involvement → high risk of ocular zoster → emergent ophthalmology consultation. In immunosuppressed hosts: (1) disseminated VZV (visceral organs, pneumonitis, encephalitis) — occurs in <4% of immunocompetent but much higher in transplant patients; (2) prolonged/atypical course with higher PHN rate. IV acyclovir (not oral) for immunocompromised zoster.
💡 Teaching Pearl
Hutchinson's sign is a physical exam pearl that every PA must know — it predicts eye involvement with near-certainty. The immunosuppressed host teaching is the advanced layer: oral antiviral is insufficient (use IV acyclovir), dissemination risk is real, and ophthalmology is not optional. This vignette rewards students who know the complications, not just the diagnosis.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 28-year-old backpacker returns from a 6-week trip through Southeast Asia. For 3 weeks he has had explosive, foul-smelling, greasy diarrhea with bloating and flatulence. He has lost 8 pounds. He has no fever and no blood in the stool.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What is the most likely organism, how do you diagnose it most efficiently, and what is the treatment?
✓ Answer
Giardia lamblia — greasy/fatty steatorrhea + no fever + no blood = classic. Diagnosis: stool antigen test (EIA or immunochromatographic — sensitivity >90%, faster than O&P). Treatment: metronidazole 250mg TID × 5-7 days OR tinidazole 2g × 1 dose (preferred for adherence).
💡 Teaching Pearl
Travel + fatty/greasy diarrhea without blood or fever = Giardia pattern recognition. The contrast with Entamoeba (bloody diarrhea + fever + liver abscess) is high-yield. Stool antigen test is now preferred over O&P for Giardia specifically because it's faster and more sensitive. Tinidazole's single-dose advantage is a prescribing pearl.
🟡 INTERMEDIATE
A 2-year-old unvaccinated child has 4 days of high fever (40°C), cough, runny nose, and red eyes. Today the mother notices small white spots inside the cheeks and a new rash starting behind the ears.
⏸ Pause & Discuss — Before Revealing
What are the white spots, what is the diagnosis, and what are the two most important complications to counsel parents about?
✓ Answer
Koplik spots (pathognomonic for measles — appear BEFORE the rash). Measles (rubeola). Rash: starts behind ears/hairline → cephalocaudal spread. Two most important complications: (1) Pneumonia (most common cause of death — 'giant cell' pneumonia), (2) SSPE (subacute sclerosing panencephalitis — fatal CNS degeneration 7-10 years later). Secondary bacterial otitis media is the most common complication overall.
💡 Teaching Pearl
The temporal sequence of measles is testable: Koplik spots appear BEFORE the rash — this is the pre-rash diagnostic window. Teaching the three complications in order of frequency vs severity is important: OtitisMedia (most common), pneumonia (leading cause of death), SSPE (most feared long-term). The rash direction (cephalocaudal) distinguishes measles from rubella (which fades faster) and roseola (rash appears after fever breaks).
